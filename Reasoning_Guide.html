🔢 Number Series

Find the next or missing term by identifying the underlying pattern.

Types of Series Patterns

TypeExamplePattern
Arithmetic2, 5, 8, 11, ?+3 each time → 14
Geometric3, 6, 12, 24, ?×2 each time → 48
Squares1, 4, 9, 16, ?1², 2², 3², 4² → 25
Cubes1, 8, 27, 64, ?1³, 2³, 3³, 4³ → 125
Fibonacci1, 1, 2, 3, 5, ?Sum of previous two → 8
Prime2, 3, 5, 7, 11, ?Prime numbers → 13
Alternating2, 6, 4, 12, 6, ?Odd: +2; Even: ×2 → 18
Difference of Diff.1, 2, 4, 8, 13, ?Diff: 1,2,4,5 → +6 → 19
Mixed ±×5, 6, 10, 11, 15, ?+1, +4, +1, +4 → 16

Key Approach Steps

  • First check simple difference (AP series)
  • Then check ratio (GP series)
  • Check if terms are squares, cubes, or primes
  • Try difference of differences (2nd-order AP)
  • Look for alternating two sub-series
  • Check n² + k or n² − k patterns
Quick Tip: For SSC/Railway, 80% of series questions use difference, squares, or Fibonacci-type patterns. Always check 2nd-order differences first if the 1st difference is not constant.

Practice Questions

Q1. 2, 6, 18, 54, ?
A) 108B) 162C) 144D) 216
Answer: B) 162 — Each term is multiplied by 3 (Geometric series ×3: 2→6→18→54→162)
Q2. 1, 4, 9, 16, 25, ?
A) 30B) 35C) 36D) 49
Answer: C) 36 — Perfect squares: 1², 2², 3², 4², 5², 6² = 36
Q3. 7, 14, 28, 56, ?
A) 98B) 112C) 124D) 130
Answer: B) 112 — Each term is doubled (×2): 56 × 2 = 112
Q4. 3, 7, 13, 21, 31, ?
A) 41B) 43C) 45D) 47
Answer: B) 43 — Differences are 4, 6, 8, 10, 12 (AP with common difference 2): 31 + 12 = 43
Q5. 1, 1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13, ?
A) 18B) 19C) 21D) 23
Answer: C) 21 — Fibonacci sequence: each term = sum of the previous two (8 + 13 = 21)
Q6. 100, 96, 89, 79, 66, ?
A) 50B) 52C) 54D) 56
Answer: A) 50 — Differences: 4, 7, 10, 13, 16 (increasing by 3 each time): 66 − 16 = 50
Q7. 2, 3, 5, 9, 17, ?
A) 29B) 31C) 33D) 35
Answer: C) 33 — Each term = 2 × previous term − 1: 17 × 2 − 1 = 33. Differences double each time: 1, 2, 4, 8, 16

Practice Questions

Q1. Book : Library :: Fish : ?
A) WaterB) AquariumC) RiverD) Net
Answer: B) Aquarium — Books are stored/displayed in Library; Fish are stored/displayed in Aquarium
Q2. 4 : 64 :: 5 : ?
A) 25B) 100C) 125D) 75
Answer: C) 125 — 4³=64, so 5³=125
Q3. Cricket : Pitch :: Tennis : ?
A) FieldB) CourtC) RingD) Track
Answer: B) Court — Cricket is played on a Pitch; Tennis is played on a Court
Q4. ABCD : DCBA :: MNOP : ?
A) PONMB) OPNMC) NOPQD) MNOP
Answer: A) PONM — The word is reversed: ABCD→DCBA, so MNOP→PONM
Q5. Painter : Brush :: Writer : ?
A) BookB) PenC) PaperD) Ink
Answer: B) Pen — A Painter uses a Brush; a Writer uses a Pen (tool of the trade)

🏷️ Classification / Odd One Out

Identify the term that does not belong to the group based on a common property.

Common Classification Bases

  • Shape/Type: Square, Rectangle, Triangle, Circle → Circle (no sides)
  • Category: Rose, Lotus, Jasmine, Mango → Mango (fruit, not flower)
  • Number property: 2, 3, 5, 7, 9 → 9 (not prime)
  • Profession: Doctor, Engineer, Teacher, Laptop → Laptop (not a person)
  • Planet vs non-planet: Mars, Jupiter, Sun, Saturn → Sun (star)
  • Alphabetical position: AZ, BY, CX, DW, EU → EU (not mirror-reversal)
Tip: Look for the MOST specific common property. If three terms share two properties and one shares only one, the one-property term is the odd one out.

Practice Questions

Q1. Find the odd one out: Mango, Apple, Banana, Potato
A) MangoB) AppleC) BananaD) Potato
Answer: D) Potato — All others are fruits; Potato is a vegetable
Q2. Find the odd one out: 2, 4, 6, 9, 12
A) 2B) 4C) 9D) 12
Answer: C) 9 — All others are even numbers; 9 is odd
Q3. Find the odd one out: Doctor, Engineer, Lawyer, Hospital
A) DoctorB) EngineerC) LawyerD) Hospital
Answer: D) Hospital — All others are professions; Hospital is a place
Q4. Find the odd one out: AZ, BY, CX, DW, EW
A) AZB) BYC) EWD) DW
Answer: C) EW — In each pair, position of 1st letter + position of 2nd letter = 27 (A+Z=1+26=27, B+Y=2+25=27, C+X=3+24=27, D+W=4+23=27, but E+W=5+23=28 ≠ 27)
Q5. Find the odd one out: Square, Rectangle, Circle, Triangle
A) SquareB) RectangleC) CircleD) Triangle
Answer: C) Circle — Square, Rectangle, Triangle all have sides and angles; Circle has no sides or angles

Practice Questions

Q1. If COLD = EQNF, then WARM = ?
A) YCTSB) YCTOC) XBSOD) ZDWU
Answer: B) YCTO — Each letter +2: W→Y, A→C, R→T, M→O
Q2. If APPLE is coded as ELPPA, then MANGO is coded as?
A) OGNAMB) NAMGOC) ONGAMD) OGMAN
Answer: A) OGNAM — The word is reversed: APPLE→ELPPA, so MANGO→OGNAM
Q3. If KING = LJOH, then QUEEN = ?
A) RVFFOB) RVFEOC) RFVFOD) RVEFO
Answer: A) RVFFO — Each letter +1: Q→R, U→V, E→F, E→F, N→O
Q4. If CAT = 3-1-20, then DOG = ?
A) 4-15-7B) 4-14-7C) 5-15-7D) 4-15-6
Answer: A) 4-15-7 — Alphabet position: D=4, O=15, G=7
Q5. If 2=4, 3=9, 4=16, then 5=?
A) 10B) 20C) 25D) 30
Answer: C) 25 — Each number is squared: 2²=4, 3²=9, 4²=16, 5²=25

👨‍👩‍👧 Blood Relations

Determine family relationships from a chain of statements.

Key Relationships Chart

StatementRelationship
A is father of BB is son/daughter of A
A is mother of BB is son/daughter of A
A is brother of BB is sibling of A
A is sister of BB is sibling of A
A is husband of BB is wife of A
A is son of B's fatherA is brother of B
A is father of B's motherA is maternal grandfather of B
A is only child of B's motherA is the only sibling of B (or B)

Common Blood Relation Terms

Maternal Grandfather = Mother's Father Paternal Uncle = Father's Brother Nephew = Brother's/Sister's Son Niece = Brother's/Sister's Daughter Cousin = Uncle/Aunt's Child Brother-in-law = Spouse's Brother / Sister's Husband
Method: Draw a family tree diagram. Use △ for male, ○ for female, = for married, — for siblings, | for parent-child. Never assume gender unless stated.

Practice Questions

Q1. A is father of B. C is mother of D. A and C are married. How is B related to D?
A) CousinB) SiblingC) NephewD) Uncle
Answer: B) Sibling — A (father) married to C (mother), so B and D are children of same parents = siblings
Q2. X is Y's brother. Y is Z's sister. Z is P's son. How is X related to P?
A) NephewB) SonC) CousinD) Grandson
Answer: B) Son — Z is P's son; Y is Z's sister (so Y is also P's child); X is Y's brother (so X is also P's child = P's son)
Q3. Pointing to Rama, Hari says, "His mother is the only daughter of my mother." How is Hari related to Rama?
A) UncleB) BrotherC) FatherD) Grandfather
Answer: A) Uncle — Only daughter of Hari's mother = Hari's sister. So Rama's mother = Hari's sister. Therefore Hari is Rama's maternal uncle
Q4. Seema's brother's only sister's mother's husband is Ram. How is Ram related to Seema?
A) BrotherB) UncleC) FatherD) Grandfather
Answer: C) Father — Seema's brother → his only sister = Seema herself → Seema's mother → Seema's mother's husband = Ram = Seema's Father
Q5. If A + B means A is father of B, A – B means A is sister of B, then P + Q – R means?
A) P is grandfather of RB) P is uncle of RC) P is father of RD) R is P's nephew
Answer: C) P is father of R — P+Q = P is Q's father; Q-R = Q is R's sister. So P is father of Q (who is R's sister) → P is father of R

🧭 Direction & Distance

Track a person's movement on a map to find final direction or total distance.

Compass Directions

North (N) ↑ West (W) ← + → East (E) ↓ South (S) NE = 45° NW = 135° SE = 315° SW = 225°

Right/Left Turn Rules

FacingAfter Right TurnAfter Left Turn
NorthEastWest
EastSouthNorth
SouthWestEast
WestNorthSouth

Shadow Rules

  • Morning (Sunrise): Shadow falls towards WEST
  • Evening (Sunset): Shadow falls towards EAST
  • Noon: Shadow falls directly downward (minimal)
Distance Tip: If movement is in opposite directions (N then S), subtract. If perpendicular, use Pythagoras: d = √(a² + b²). Draw the path on rough paper — it saves time.

Practice Questions

Q1. Ram walks 10km North, turns East and walks 6km, then turns South and walks 10km. How far and in which direction is he from start?
A) 6km WestB) 10km SouthC) 6km EastD) 16km East
Answer: C) 6km East — North 10 and South 10 cancel out; East 6 remains → 6km East of start
Q2. A man walks 5km East, 3km North, 5km West. Where is he from start?
A) 3km NorthB) 3km SouthC) 5km WestD) 5km North
Answer: A) 3km North — East 5 and West 5 cancel; only North 3 remains → 3km North
Q3. A and B start from same point. A walks 4km North, B walks 3km East. What is the straight-line distance between them?
A) 7kmB) 5kmC) 6kmD) 4km
Answer: B) 5km — Pythagoras: √(4²+3²) = √(16+9) = √25 = 5km
Q4. If you face North and turn 90° clockwise, then 180° clockwise, which direction do you face?
A) NorthB) SouthC) EastD) West
Answer: D) West — N + 90° CW = East; East + 180° CW = West
Q5. At 9 AM, the shadow of a pole falls towards the West. In which direction is the sun?
A) WestB) SouthC) EastD) North
Answer: C) East — Morning sun is in the East; shadows fall opposite to the sun's direction = West

🏆 Order & Ranking

Find position from top or bottom using total count.

Key Formulas

Total = Rank from Top + Rank from Bottom − 1
Rank from Bottom = Total − Rank from Top + 1
Rank from Top = Total − Rank from Bottom + 1

Number of Persons Between Two

Between A and B = |Rank(A) − Rank(B)| − 1 (from same end)
Between A and B = Total − Rank(A) − Rank(B) + 1 (from opposite ends)

Example

Reena is 15th from top and 30th from bottom.
Total students = 15 + 30 − 1 = 44
In a row, A is 5th from left, B is 8th from right. If there are 3 persons between them and A is to the left of B, Total = 5 + 3 + 8 = 16 persons.

Practice Questions

Q1. Ravi ranks 10th from top in a class of 40. What is his rank from bottom?
A) 30B) 31C) 29D) 32
Answer: B) 31 — Rank from bottom = Total − Rank from top + 1 = 40 − 10 + 1 = 31
Q2. Seema is 5th from top and 25th from bottom. How many students are there?
A) 30B) 28C) 29D) 31
Answer: C) 29 — Total = Rank from top + Rank from bottom − 1 = 5 + 25 − 1 = 29
Q3. In a row of 30 people, A is 12th from left and B is 8th from right. How many people are between them?
A) 9B) 11C) 10D) 12
Answer: C) 10 — People between = Total − (Rank from left + Rank from right) = 30 − (12+8) = 10
Q4. Anil is 6 ranks ahead of Sunil in a class of 45. If Sunil's rank is 25 from the top, what is Anil's rank from bottom?
A) 26B) 27C) 25D) 32
Answer: B) 27 — Anil rank from top = 25−6=19; Rank from bottom = 45−19+1 = 27

💺 Seating Arrangement

Place people in linear rows, circles, or rectangular tables based on given clues.

Types

Linear (1 row): People face North. Left = West side, Right = East side. Relative to the person, not the observer.
Linear (2 rows): Two facing rows. Person in Row 1 faces South; in Row 2 faces North. "Immediate left" depends on which row and direction.
Circular (Inward): All face the centre. Right of a person = anticlockwise direction when viewed from above.
Circular (Outward): All face away from centre. Right = clockwise direction.

Solving Steps

  • Identify the type (linear/circular) and direction people face
  • Start with definite clues (fixed positions)
  • Use "not adjacent" or "exactly between" clues to eliminate
  • Draw a diagram and fill positions step by step
  • Verify all clues with your final arrangement
Exam Tip: In circular arrangements, if persons face the centre, "A sits to the left of B" means A is in the anticlockwise direction from B.

Practice Questions

Q1. Six persons A, B, C, D, E, F sit in a circle facing the centre. A sits opposite D, B sits between A and C. Who sits to the immediate right of A?
A) BB) CC) ED) F
Answer: A) B — B is between A and C, and in a circle facing centre, right of A = the person in clockwise direction; B sits adjacent to A (between A and C), so B is to the right of A
Q2. In a linear row of 7 people facing North, P is 3rd from left, Q is 5th from left. How many people sit between P and Q?
A) 1B) 2C) 3D) 0
Answer: A) 1 — P is at position 3, Q at position 5; between positions 3 and 5 is only position 4 → 1 person between them
Q3. In a two-row arrangement, Row 1 faces South and Row 2 faces North. A (Row 1) faces B (Row 2). Who sits to A's left?
A) Person to the East of AB) Person to the West of AC) Person directly behind AD) Cannot determine
Answer: A) Person to the East of A — Row 1 faces South. When facing South, East is to the left (since left of South-facing = East direction)
Q4. In a circular arrangement of 6 people (A,B,C,D,E,F), A is 3rd to the right of D. B is 2nd to the left of A. How many people are between B and D (going clockwise)?
A) 0B) 1C) 2D) 3
Answer: C) 2 — D→3 right→A (positions: D=1,_=2,_=3,A=4 clockwise). B is 2 left of A → B is at position 2. Clockwise from B(2) to D(1): goes through 3,4,5,6,1 = 4 people, but anticlockwise B(2) to D(1) = 0; between them clockwise = 5-1=2 others between B and D

🔵 Syllogism

Use Venn diagrams to test whether a conclusion follows from given statements.

Statement Types & Venn Diagrams

StatementVenn DiagramMeaning
All A are BA ⊂ B (A inside B)Every member of A is in B
No A is BA ∩ B = ∅ (separate)A and B have no common element
Some A are BA ∩ B ≠ ∅ (overlapping)At least one element is common
Some A are not BPart of A outside BAt least one element of A is not in B

Key Rules

  • "All A are B" + "All B are C" → "All A are C" ✅
  • "All A are B" + "Some B are C" → "Some A are C" ✗ (not definite)
  • "No A is B" + "All C are A" → "No C is B" ✅
  • "Some A are B" + "Some B are C" → No direct conclusion
  • Possibility conclusions are valid if not contradicted
New Pattern: In newer exams (SSC CGL, IBPS PO), "Either-Or" conclusions are tested. "Either Conclusion I or II follows" is correct when neither follows individually but together they cover all cases.

Practice Questions

Q1. Statements: All dogs are animals. All animals are living beings. Conclusions: I. All dogs are living beings. II. Some living beings are dogs.
A) Only IB) Only IIC) Both I & IID) Neither
Answer: C) Both I & II — All dogs are animals + All animals are living beings → All dogs are living beings (I). If all dogs are living beings, some living beings are dogs (II follows by conversion)
Q2. Statements: Some cats are dogs. All dogs are pets. Conclusions: I. Some cats are pets. II. All cats are pets.
A) Only IB) Only IIC) BothD) Neither
Answer: A) Only I follows — Some cats are dogs + All dogs are pets → Some cats are pets (I). We cannot say ALL cats are pets since only SOME cats are dogs (II fails)
Q3. Statements: No rose is a flower. All flowers are red. Conclusions: I. No rose is red. II. Some red things are flowers.
A) Only IB) Only IIC) BothD) Neither
Answer: B) Only II follows — All flowers are red → Some red things are flowers (II follows). I fails because roses may still be red through another connection
Q4. Statements: Some mangoes are fruits. All fruits are healthy. Conclusions: I. Some mangoes are healthy. II. All healthy things are mangoes.
A) Only IB) Only IIC) BothD) Neither
Answer: A) Only I follows — Some mangoes are fruits + All fruits are healthy → Some mangoes are healthy (I). II fails (healthy is a broader category than mangoes)

📋 Statement & Conclusions / Assumptions / Courses of Action

Evaluate logical validity of conclusions and assumptions from given statements.

Types

TypeKey Rule
ConclusionsMust follow directly from the statement. No external knowledge needed.
AssumptionsMust be implicit (taken for granted) in the statement, not explicitly stated.
Courses of ActionMust be practical, directly address the problem, and be implementable.
Arguments (Strong/Weak)Strong: directly relevant & important. Weak: trivial, dubious, or unrelated.

Common Traps

  • A conclusion that is "probably true" but not definite → NOT a valid conclusion
  • An assumption that restates the statement → NOT implicit (it's explicit)
  • A course of action that is too drastic or involves external parties → usually weak
  • Emotional arguments without logical basis → Weak arguments

Practice Questions

Q1. Statement: "Our products are better than those of competitors." Conclusion: I. Competitors' products are inferior. II. Our products are the best in the world.
A) Only I followsB) Only II followsC) Both followD) Neither follows
Answer: A) Only I follows — I directly restates the statement in different words (follows). II is too extreme — "better than competitors" does not mean best in the world
Q2. Statement: "The government has decided to increase petrol prices." Assumption: I. People will reduce petrol usage. II. The government needs more revenue.
A) Only IB) Only IIC) BothD) Neither
Answer: D) Neither is implicit — Neither assumption is taken for granted in the statement. The decision may be for various reasons not stated; both assumptions bring in external reasoning not implicit in the statement
Q3. Statement: "Ravi was caught stealing in the market." Course of Action: I. He should be handed to police. II. The market should be closed immediately.
A) Only IB) Only IIC) BothD) Neither
Answer: A) Only I follows — Handing to police is a practical, direct action. Closing the market is too drastic and doesn't address the problem

🧩 Puzzles

Multi-variable arrangement questions combining seating, floors, months, and schedules.

Common Puzzle Types

Floor-Based Month/Year Scheduling Box Stacking Double-Row Arrangement Designation + City Color + Object

Step-by-Step Method

  • List all variables (persons, floors, colors, months, etc.)
  • Create a table with columns for each variable
  • Fill in definite clues first (exact positions)
  • Use elimination for "not" clues
  • Try conditional clues with both possibilities
  • Verify the final solution against all clues
Exam Tip (IBPS/SBI): Puzzle sets typically contain 5 questions from one set. Solving the arrangement once gives all 5 marks. Prioritize puzzles over single-question types.

Practice Questions

Q1. Five people A, B, C, D, E live on floors 1–5 (1=lowest). D lives on an even-numbered floor. A lives above D. B lives on floor 1. C lives between A and D. Who lives on floor 3?
A) AB) CC) DD) E
Answer: B) C — B=floor 1, D on even floor: D=2 or 4. A above D, C between A and D. If D=2, A=4 or 5, C=3. If D=4, A=5, C between 4 and 5 impossible. So D=2, A=4 or 5, C=3
Q2. In a puzzle, P, Q, R, S are born in Jan, Mar, Jun, Sep (not necessarily in order). P is born before Q. R is born in the last month among them. S is not born in Jan. Who is born in Jan?
A) PB) QC) RD) S
Answer: A) P — R is born in Sep (last). S is not born in Jan. P is born before Q. The only person who can be in Jan (first month) is P
Q3. Six boxes are stacked 1(bottom) to 6(top). Box A is immediately above Box B. Box C is 3rd from top. Box D is between B and C. Which box is at the bottom?
A) AB) BC) CD) D
Answer: B) B — C is 3rd from top = position 4. D is between B and C. A is immediately above B. So order (bottom up): B, A, ..., D, C (4th). For D between B and C: B lowest, then A above B, D above A, C at 4

🔲 Pattern Completion

Identify the missing figure to complete the visual sequence or matrix.

Approach

  • Look at rows and columns for rotation, reflection, or addition of elements
  • Check if number of elements increases or decreases in a pattern
  • Identify shading patterns (diagonal, alternating, progressive fill)
  • Check if figures rotate clockwise (90°, 180°, 270°)
  • In 3×3 matrices, the third row/column is often the XOR/combination of first two
Time Tip: Non-verbal questions are visual — eliminate obviously wrong options first. Usually 2-3 options can be eliminated based on one obvious feature (shape, number of sides, shading).

Practice Questions

Q1. In a 3×3 matrix where each row contains ○, △, □ in some order, and each column also contains each shape once, what shape goes in the bottom-right cell if top row is ○△□ and middle row is △□○?
A) ○B) △C) □D) Cannot determine
Answer: B) △ — Bottom row must be □○△ (each shape once per row). Right column has □,○,? → must be △ to complete all three shapes
Q2. A figure rotates 90° clockwise in each step. If at step 1 an arrow points North (↑), at step 4 it points?
A) NorthB) SouthC) EastD) West
Answer: D) West — Step 1: N(↑), Step 2: E(→), Step 3: S(↓), Step 4: W(←). Each 90° CW rotation: N→E→S→W
Q3. In a series of figures, the number of dots increases: 1, 3, 6, 10, ?
A) 12B) 13C) 15D) 16
Answer: C) 15 — Triangular numbers: differences are 2,3,4,5 → next difference = 5, so 10+5=15

Find the correct reflection of a figure or word along a vertical or horizontal axis.

Mirror Image (Vertical Mirror on Right)

Original: b d p q → Mirror: d b q p Left↔Right swapped. Up/Down unchanged.

Water Image (Horizontal Mirror Below)

Original: A B C → Water Image looks like ∀ ᴮ Ͻ Up↔Down swapped. Left/Right unchanged.

Clock Mirror: If a clock shows 3:15, its mirror image shows 8:45. Formula: Mirror time = 11:60 − original time (or 12:00 − original if exact hour).

Practice Questions

Q1. What is the mirror image of the word "REASON" when placed in front of a vertical mirror?
A) REASONB) NOSAERC) ЯƎAƧOИ (reversed)D) Same but upside down
Answer: C) Each letter is laterally reversed — Vertical mirror reverses left↔right. Each letter appears flipped horizontally (R→Я, E→Ǝ etc.). The word order appears reversed to a reader facing the mirror
Q2. A clock shows 9:25. What time does it show in its mirror image?
A) 2:35B) 3:35C) 2:45D) 3:25
Answer: A) 2:35 — Formula: 11:60 − 9:25 = 2:35
Q3. Which letters look the same in their water image (horizontal mirror)?
A) B and DB) C and DC) H and XD) P and Q
Answer: C) H and X — Letters with horizontal symmetry look the same in water image: H, I, O, X have horizontal symmetry (top half = mirror of bottom half)
Q4. A figure has a triangle pointing up (△). What is its water image?
A) △ (same)B) ▽ (pointing down)C) ◁ (pointing left)D) ▷ (pointing right)

🔍 Embedded Figures

Identify which answer figure contains the given question figure as a part.

Approach

  • Mentally rotate the question figure and look for it inside each option
  • Focus on distinctive features: unusual angles, specific proportions, unique shapes
  • The embedded figure must retain the same shape and size (only orientation may change)
  • Lines of the embedded figure should not be broken by the host figure's lines
Tip: Start by checking if the number of sides/lines in the question figure matches what's visible in each option. Eliminate options that don't have enough matching line segments.

Practice Questions

Q1. A triangle (△) is the question figure. Which of the following complex figures would most likely contain it as an embedded figure?
A) A circleB) A rectangle divided diagonallyC) A circle with a dotD) A square with a cross inside
Answer: B) A rectangle divided diagonally — A diagonal line across a rectangle creates two triangles; the triangle (△) is embedded within it. Options A, C, D have no triangular regions
Q2. How many triangles are there in a Star of David (✡) figure?
A) 6B) 8C) 12D) 2
Answer: C) 12 — A Star of David has 6 small outer triangles + 6 larger triangles formed by overlapping = 12 triangles in total (including various sizes)
Q3. In a square divided into 4 equal parts by horizontal and vertical lines, how many rectangles (including squares) are embedded in total?
A) 4B) 6C) 8D) 9
Answer: D) 9 — Formula for m×n grid: (m+1)C2 × (n+1)C2 = 3C2 × 3C2 = 3 × 3 = 9 rectangles

📄 Paper Folding & Cutting

Determine how a paper looks after folding, punching holes, and unfolding.

Rules

  • When folded and punched, the hole appears symmetrically on both halves
  • Number of holes after unfolding = 2ⁿ where n = number of folds
  • For a single fold: 1 punch → 2 holes; For 2 folds: 1 punch → 4 holes
  • The position of holes is the mirror image across the fold line

Common Fold Types

Left half over Right Right half over Left Top half over Bottom Bottom half over Top Diagonal fold
Method: Trace the fold mentally. Imagine the top sheet as transparent. Where the punch lands on both top and bottom layers are where holes appear after unfolding.

📝 Practice Questions

Q1. A square paper is folded in half (left half over right). One hole is punched. How many holes appear after unfolding?
A) 1B) 2C) 3D) 4
Answer: B) 2 — One fold → 2¹ = 2 holes; the punch passes through both layers, creating a symmetrical hole on each side of the fold
Q2. A paper is folded twice (once horizontally, once vertically) and one hole is punched. How many holes appear after unfolding?
A) 2B) 3C) 4D) 8
Answer: C) 4 — Two folds → 2² = 4 holes; each fold doubles the number of holes because the punch goes through all stacked layers
Q3. A rectangular paper is folded so that the right edge meets the left edge. A cut is made at the top-right corner. After unfolding, what does the paper look like?
A) One cut at top-right onlyB) Symmetric cuts at both top cornersC) Cut at the centre onlyD) No cut visible
Answer: B) Symmetric cuts at both top corners — Folding aligns both layers; cutting one corner cuts the same position on both layers, creating symmetric notches at both top corners when unfolded

🎲 Cube & Dice

Questions on painted cubes, dice face identification, and dice unfolding.

Painted Cube Formula

A cube of side n painted on all faces is cut into unit cubes:

Dice Rules

  • A standard die: 1+6=7, 2+5=7, 3+4=7 (opposite faces sum to 7)
  • When two dice are shown, find the common face — that face connects the two positions
  • If two faces are adjacent in one view, they CANNOT be opposite faces
  • When a die is rolled, the top face changes but relationship of other faces stays constant
Unfolding Tip: There are 11 ways to unfold a cube. The key rule: faces directly opposite each other in an unfolded cross are the OPPOSITE faces of the cube.

📝 Practice Questions

Q1. A cube painted red on all faces is cut into 27 equal cubes (3×3×3). How many smaller cubes have exactly 2 faces painted?
A) 6B) 12C) 8D) 24
Answer: B) 12 — Edge cubes (2 faces painted) = 12(n−2) = 12(3−2) = 12. These are the cubes sitting on each edge of the large cube
Q2. A cube painted on all faces is cut into 64 equal cubes (4×4×4). How many smaller cubes have no face painted?
A) 4B) 8C) 16D) 24
Answer: B) 8 — Inside cubes (no face painted) = (n−2)³ = (4−2)³ = 2³ = 8
Q3. On a standard die, 1 is opposite 6, 2 is opposite 5, 3 is opposite 4. If 3 is on top and 1 faces you, which number is on the right?
A) 2B) 4C) 5D) 6
Answer: A) 2 — 3 on top → 4 on bottom; 1 facing you → 6 at the back; remaining faces 2 and 5 are on left/right. By standard orientation, 2 is on the right

🗂️ Figure Matrix

Find the missing figure in a 3×3 or 2×2 matrix using row/column rules.

Common Patterns in Matrices

  • Each row/column has the same number of elements but arranged differently
  • Figures rotate 90° or 180° across rows/columns
  • Shading pattern alternates or progresses across the matrix
  • The third figure = combination (OR/XOR) of first two
  • One attribute changes per row, another per column
Strategy: Identify 2-3 consistent rules in the top two rows, then apply them to find the missing figure in row 3. The correct answer must satisfy ALL rules, not just one.

📝 Practice Questions

Q1. In a 3×3 matrix: Row 1: ○ △ □ | Row 2: △ □ ○ | Row 3: □ ○ ? — What fills the missing cell?
A) ○B) △C) □D) Any shape
Answer: B) △ — Each row and column must contain ○, △, □ exactly once. Row 3 already has □ and ○, so △ is missing. Column 3 also has □ and ○, confirming △
Q2. In a figure matrix, each column shows shapes with increasing sides (3→4→5). Middle row, column 2 has a square (4 sides). What is in middle row, column 3?
A) TriangleB) SquareC) PentagonD) Hexagon
Answer: C) Pentagon — Pattern: col 1 = triangle (3 sides), col 2 = square (4 sides), col 3 = pentagon (5 sides); this rule applies to every row
Q3. A 2×2 matrix has: top-left = filled circle, top-right = empty circle, bottom-left = filled square. What is the bottom-right figure?
A) Filled circleB) Empty squareC) Filled squareD) Empty triangle
Answer: B) Empty square — Rule: left column = filled, right column = empty; top row = circles, bottom row = squares. Bottom-right = empty square

≥ Inequalities / Coded Inequalities

Decode symbols and determine the correct relationship between elements.

Symbol Decoding

Symbol UsedMeaning
@ or ©Greater than (>)
% or $Less than (<)
# or *Greater than or equal to (≥)
^ or &Less than or equal to (≤)
! or ~Equal to (=)

Chain Inequality Rules

  • A > B > C → A > C (transitive) ✅
  • A > B = C → A > C ✅
  • A > B and B < C → No definite relation between A and C ✗
  • A ≥ B > C → A > C ✅
  • Either-Or: When neither I nor II follows but together they form a complete comparison
Key: Always decode first, then form the chain. If the chain has a "break" (one > then one <), you cannot draw a definite conclusion between the ends.

📝 Practice Questions

Q1. If P > Q = R < S, which conclusion is definitely true?
A) P > SB) S > QC) P > RD) S = P
Answer: C) P > R — P > Q and Q = R → P > R (transitive). We cannot compare P and S because the chain breaks at R < S
Q2. If A ≥ B > C = D, which conclusions follow? I) A > D II) B ≥ D
A) Only IB) Only IIC) Both I and IID) Neither
Answer: C) Both I and II — A ≥ B > C = D → A > D (I follows). B > C = D → B > D, so B ≥ D is also true (II follows)
Q3. If P ≥ Q > R ≤ S = T, which conclusions follow? I) P > R II) T ≥ R
A) Only IB) Only IIC) Both I and IID) Neither
Answer: C) Both I and II — P ≥ Q > R → P > R (I follows). R ≤ S = T → T ≥ R (II follows)
Q4. Coded: A @ B = A > B; A # B = A = B; A % B = A < B. If P @ Q % R # S, which is true?
A) P > SB) P < RC) Q < SD) Cannot determine P vs S
Answer: D) Cannot determine P vs S — Decoded: P > Q < R = S. Chain breaks at Q: P > Q but Q < R = S, so no definite relation between P and S

🔡 Alphanumeric Series

Mixed series of letters, numbers, and symbols — find position, count, or next term.

Types of Questions

  • Position-based: "What is the 5th element from the left?" or "Which element is 3rd to the right of M?"
  • Count-based: "How many letters are between two specific positions?"
  • Condition-based: "How many vowels are immediately followed by a number?"
  • Rearrangement: "If all symbols are removed, what is the 6th from right?"

Approach

  • Write the series with position numbers below each element
  • For "Xth from right" questions: Position from left = Total − X + 1
  • Mark vowels, consonants, numbers, symbols separately if needed
  • Re-number after removal/rearrangement before answering

📝 Practice Questions

Q1. Series: A, 2, C, 4, E, 6, G, ? — What comes next?
A) HB) 7C) 8D) I
Answer: C) 8 — Alternating pattern: odd positions = letters (A,C,E,G — every other letter); even positions = even numbers (2,4,6,8). Next term at even position = 8
Q2. Series: B, F, J, N, ? — What comes next?
A) PB) QC) RD) S
Answer: C) R — Each letter jumps +4 positions: B(2)→F(6)→J(10)→N(14)→R(18)
Q3. Series: AZ, BY, CX, DW, ? — What comes next?
A) EVB) EWC) FVD) EU
Answer: A) EV — First letter goes forward (A,B,C,D,E); second letter goes backward (Z,Y,X,W,V). Next pair = EV
Q4. Series: Z, X, V, T, ? — What comes next?
A) SB) RC) QD) P
Answer: B) R — Each letter moves back by 2: Z(26)→X(24)→V(22)→T(20)→R(18)

🕐 Clock & Calendar

Clock Angle Formula

Angle between hands = |30H − 5.5M| (H = hours, M = minutes)
Hands coincide: Every 65.45 minutes (12 times in 12 hours)
Hands at 90°: Every 32.72 minutes (44 times in 24 hours)
Hands at 180° (straight): Every 65.45 minutes (11 times in 12 hours)

Faulty Clock

Correct time = Given time × (Correct rate / Faulty rate) If clock gains X min in Y hours, it runs fast.

Calendar — Day Calculation

Odd Days: Remainder when days are divided by 7.
Ordinary year = 1 odd day. Leap year = 2 odd days.
Century odd days: 100 yrs = 5, 200 yrs = 3, 300 yrs = 1, 400 yrs = 0.

Leap Year Rules

  • Divisible by 4 → Leap year (e.g., 2024)
  • Divisible by 100 but NOT 400 → NOT a leap year (e.g., 1900)
  • Divisible by 400 → Leap year (e.g., 2000)

📝 Practice Questions

Q1. What is the angle between the hands of a clock at 3:30?
A) 90°B) 75°C) 105°D) 60°
Answer: B) 75° — Formula: |30H − 5.5M| = |30×3 − 5.5×30| = |90 − 165| = 75°
Q2. What is the angle between the hands of a clock at 8:20?
A) 110°B) 130°C) 150°D) 120°
Answer: B) 130° — |30×8 − 5.5×20| = |240 − 110| = 130°
Q3. Jan 1, 2024 is Monday (2024 is a leap year). What day is Jan 1, 2025?
A) MondayB) WednesdayC) TuesdayD) Thursday
Answer: B) Wednesday — 2024 is a leap year = 366 days = 52 weeks + 2 odd days. Monday + 2 = Wednesday
Q4. How many times do the hands of a clock coincide in 12 hours?
A) 12 timesB) 11 timesC) 10 timesD) 22 times
Answer: B) 11 times — Hands meet every 65.45 min; in 720 min: 720 ÷ 65.45 ≈ 11 times (they do NOT coincide at 12:00 a second time within the 12-hour cycle)

⚡ Tricks & Shortcuts — All Topics

Master these speed tricks to solve reasoning questions 2x faster in exams.

Series Tricks

Difference Check: Always subtract consecutive terms first. If differences are constant → AP. If differences themselves form a pattern → 2nd order.
Squares Near: For series like 2, 5, 10, 17, 26 → these are n²+1 (1²+1=2, 2²+1=5…). Always check n²±k.

Coding Shortcuts

Write A=1…Z=26 on your rough sheet immediately. For +3 shift, just add 3 to each position and convert back.
Reverse Alphabet: A↔Z, B↔Y means position + mirror = 27. So C(3) mirrors X(24) because 3+24=27.

Direction Shortcuts

Opposite directions cancel: Walk 4km N then 4km S = net 0km. Walk 3km E then 4km N = √(3²+4²) = 5km from start.
Shadow direction: Morning sun rises in East → shadow is West. Draw the sun position first, then the shadow falls opposite.

Blood Relation Shortcuts

Gender trick: If the problem says "X's only child is Y" and asks about Y's relation to X — Y can be son OR daughter unless gender is given. Mark as "child of X".

Ranking Shortcuts

If positions overlap: Minimum people = (Rank from top) + (Rank from bottom) − 1 If positions don't overlap: Minimum people = Rank from top + Rank from bottom
