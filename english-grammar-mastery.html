Grammar Basics

Parts of Speech

The 9 building blocks of every English sentence — understand each one with clear definitions and examples.

Noun (naming word)

A noun is a word that names a person, place, thing, animal, quality, or idea.

Definition: A noun is the name of any person, place, thing, or idea.

Types of Nouns

TypeDefinitionExamples
Proper NounName of a specific person/placeIndia, Ravi, Amazon
Common NounGeneral name for a classcity, boy, river
Collective NounGroup of persons/thingsteam, flock, army
Abstract NounIdea, quality, or statelove, freedom, courage
Material NounSubstance or materialgold, water, wood
Countable NounCan be countedbook, chair, apple
Uncountable NounCannot be countedmilk, air, advice
Examples in sentences:
• Mumbai is a busy city. (Proper + Common)
• Her courage inspired the whole team. (Abstract + Collective)
• The water in this bottle is pure. (Material + Countable)

Quick Noun Rules

  • Proper nouns always start with a capital letter.
  • Collective nouns can take singular or plural verbs depending on context.
  • Abstract nouns are usually uncountable (love, honesty).
  • Some nouns have irregular plurals: child→children, man→men, foot→feet.
  • Compound nouns: toothbrush, swimming pool, mother-in-law.

Noun Gender

Common Mistake: Do NOT use "a" before uncountable nouns. Say advice, not an advice. Say information, not an information.

Pronoun (replacing word)

A pronoun is a word used in place of a noun to avoid repetition.

TypeExamples
PersonalI, you, he, she, it, we, they
Possessivemy, your, his, her, its, our, their
Reflexivemyself, yourself, himself, herself, itself
Demonstrativethis, that, these, those
Interrogativewho, whom, which, what, whose
Relativewho, which, that, whom, whose
Indefiniteall, each, some, none, anyone, nobody
Reciprocaleach other, one another
Pronoun Case:
• Subjective: I, he, she, we, they
• Objective: me, him, her, us, them
• Possessive: mine, his, hers, ours, theirs

Examples & Usage

  • She is a brilliant scientist. (Personal)
  • The book lost its cover. (Possessive)
  • He hurt himself while playing. (Reflexive)
  • This is my favourite movie. (Demonstrative)
  • Who called you last night? (Interrogative)
  • The man who arrived is my uncle. (Relative)
  • Everyone must follow the rules. (Indefinite)
  • They greeted each other warmly. (Reciprocal)
Common Mistakes:
✗ Between you and I → ✓ Between you and me
✗ Its raining → ✓ It's raining (It's = It is)
✗ Me and John went → ✓ John and I went

Adjective (describing word)

An adjective modifies or describes a noun or pronoun.

TypeExamples
Descriptivebeautiful, tall, heavy, cold
Numeralone, first, many, few
Demonstrativethis, that, these, those
Possessivemy, your, his, her, our
Interrogativewhich, what, whose
Distributiveeach, every, either, neither
ProperIndian, French, American

Degrees of Comparison

Examples & Tips

  • She has a beautiful smile. (Descriptive)
  • Give me three books. (Numeral)
  • I want that red dress. (Demonstrative)
  • Every student must attend. (Distributive)
  • He is taller than his brother. (Comparative)
  • She is the most intelligent student. (Superlative)

Example: a lovely little old rectangular green French silver whittling knife
Common Mistake:
✗ She is more taller → ✓ She is taller
✗ This is the most best → ✓ This is the best

Adverb (modifying word)

An adverb modifies a verb, adjective, or another adverb — telling how, when, where, or to what extent.

TypeQuestion AnsweredExamples
MannerHow?quickly, slowly, well, badly
TimeWhen?now, then, today, soon, already
PlaceWhere?here, there, away, inside, outside
FrequencyHow often?always, often, never, rarely, daily
DegreeHow much?very, quite, almost, enough, too
ReasonWhy?therefore, hence, consequently

Adverb Examples

  • She sings beautifully. (Manner)
  • He will come tomorrow. (Time)
  • The cat sat there. (Place)
  • I always brush my teeth. (Frequency)
  • This is very important. (Degree)
Most adverbs of manner are formed by adding -ly to adjectives:
slow→slowly, quick→quickly, happy→happily, easy→easily
Adjective vs Adverb:
• He is a quick runner. (adjective — describes noun)
• He runs quickly. (adverb — describes verb)
• She felt bad. (adjective after linking verb, NOT badly)

Preposition

A preposition shows the relationship of a noun/pronoun to another word — indicating position, direction, time, or manner.

TypePrepositions
Place/Positionin, on, at, under, over, between, beside
Timeat, on, in, before, after, during, since, for
Directionto, from, into, through, towards, across
Mannerwith, by, like, as
Agentby, with
AT / IN / ON (Time):
• AT: specific time — at 5 o'clock, at noon, at night
• ON: days/dates — on Monday, on 15 August
• IN: months/years/seasons — in March, in 2024, in winter

Tricky Prepositions

  • She lives in London. (city/country)
  • The book is on the table.
  • Meet me at the station.
  • He has been working since Monday.
  • I waited for two hours.
  • She is afraid of darkness.
  • He is good at maths.
  • She is interested in music.
Common Mistakes:
✗ Discuss about → ✓ Discuss (no 'about')
✗ Return back → ✓ Return (back is redundant)
✗ Cope up with → ✓ Cope with

Conjunction (joining word)

A conjunction joins words, phrases, or clauses.

TypeWordsUse
Coordinatingfor, and, nor, but, or, yet, so (FANBOYS)Joins equal parts
Subordinatingbecause, although, since, while, if, unlessJoins main+subordinate clause
Correlativeboth…and, either…or, neither…nor, not only…but alsoUsed in pairs
  • I tried hard, but I failed. (Coordinating)
  • He left because it was late. (Subordinating)
  • Neither she nor he was present. (Correlative)

Common Subordinating Conjunctions

becausesincealthough thoughwhilewhen beforeafterif unlessuntilas so thatin order thateven if
Use a comma after a subordinate clause at the start:
Although it was raining, we went out.
Common Mistake:
✗ Although he tried hard, but he failed.
✓ Although he tried hard, he failed.
✓ He tried hard, but he failed. (not both)

Interjection (exclamatory word)

An interjection expresses a sudden feeling or emotion and is grammatically independent of the rest of the sentence.

Types & Examples

EmotionInterjectionsExample
JoyHurray! Yay! Wow!Hurray! We won!
SorrowAlas! Oh! Ah!Alas! She is gone.
SurpriseOh! What! Really!Oh! Is that true?
ApprovalBravo! Well done!Bravo! Excellent work!
ContemptPooh! Bah! Fie!Pooh! What rubbish!
GreetingHello! Hi! Hey!Hello! How are you?
PainOuch! Ow!Ouch! That hurts!
AttentionHush! Look! Listen!Hush! Baby is sleeping.
Punctuation with Interjections:
• Strong emotion → exclamation mark: Wow! That's amazing!
• Mild emotion → comma: Well, I suppose so.
• Can stand alone as a sentence
Note: Interjections have no grammatical connection with the sentence. They are simply inserted to express emotion and are often followed by an exclamation mark.

Complete Guide

All 12 Tenses

Master every tense with structure, formula, positive, negative, interrogative sentences and examples.

Simple Present Tense
(+) S + V1/Vs + O  |  (-) S + do/does not + V1  |  (?) Do/Does + S + V1?
+ Positive
  • She reads books every day.
  • They play cricket on Sundays.
  • The sun rises in the east.
- Negative
  • She does not read books.
  • They do not play cricket.
  • He does not like coffee.
? Interrogative
  • Does she read books?
  • Do they play cricket?
  • Does he like coffee?
Uses: Habits, routines, universal truths, permanent states. Signal words: always, usually, often, never, every day/week
Common Mistake: He go to school daily. → He goes to school daily. (3rd person singular needs -s/es)
Present Continuous Tense
(+) S + is/am/are + V-ing + O  |  (-) S + is/am/are + not + V-ing  |  (?) Is/Am/Are + S + V-ing?
+ Positive
  • She is reading now.
  • They are playing in the park.
  • I am writing a letter.
- Negative
  • She is not reading now.
  • They are not playing outside.
? Interrogative
  • Is she reading a book?
  • Are they playing?
Uses: Actions happening right now, temporary situations, future plans. Signal words: now, at the moment, currently, right now, today
Stative verbs NOT used in continuous: know, believe, want, need, love, hate, see, hear, own, seem, contain, understand
Present Perfect Tense
(+) S + has/have + V3 + O  |  (-) S + has/have + not + V3  |  (?) Has/Have + S + V3?
+ Positive
  • She has written the report.
  • They have visited Paris.
  • I have just eaten lunch.
- Negative
  • She has not written it.
  • I have not eaten yet.
? Interrogative
  • Has she written the report?
  • Have you ever visited London?
Uses: Past action with present relevance, life experience, recently completed actions. Signal words: just, already, yet, ever, never, recently, since, for, so far
Present Perfect Continuous Tense
(+) S + has/have + been + V-ing + O  |  (?): Has/Have + S + been + V-ing + O?
+ Positive
  • She has been working since morning.
  • They have been playing for 2 hours.
- Negative
  • She has not been working today.
  • He has not been feeling well.
? Interrogative
  • How long have you been waiting?
Uses: Action started in past and continuing to present, emphasizing duration. Signal words: since, for, all day, how long, lately, recently
Simple Past Tense
(+) S + V2 + O  |  (-) S + did not + V1 + O  |  (?) Did + S + V1 + O?
+ Positive
  • She wrote a letter yesterday.
  • They played football last Sunday.
  • He went to school by bus.
- Negative
  • She did not write a letter.
  • They did not play yesterday.
? Interrogative
  • Did she write a letter?
  • Did they play football?
Uses: Completed past actions at a specific time, sequence of events, past habits. Signal words: yesterday, last night/week, ago, in 1990, then, once
Past Continuous Tense
(+) S + was/were + V-ing + O  |  (-) S + was/were + not + V-ing  |  (?) Was/Were + S + V-ing?
+ Positive
  • She was reading when he called.
  • They were playing at 5 p.m.
  • I was studying all evening.
- Negative
  • She was not reading the book.
  • He was not sleeping properly.
? Interrogative
  • Was she reading?
  • What were you doing at 9 p.m.?
Uses: Ongoing past action at a specific time, interrupted action (when/while). Signal words: at that time, when, while, as, all day yesterday
Past Perfect Tense
(+) S + had + V3 + O  |  (-) S + had not + V3  |  (?) Had + S + V3?
+ Positive
  • She had finished work before he arrived.
  • The train had left before we reached.
- Negative
  • She had not finished the work.
  • They had not met before.
? Interrogative
  • Had she finished the work?
  • Had he ever visited London?
Uses: Action completed before another past action ("the earlier of two past events"). Signal words: before, after, already, by the time, when, once
Past Perfect Continuous Tense
(+) S + had + been + V-ing + O  |  (?): Had + S + been + V-ing?
+ Positive
  • She had been working for 3 hours when he called.
  • They had been waiting for an hour.
- Negative
  • She had not been sleeping well before the exam.
? Interrogative
  • How long had he been studying?
Uses: Continuous action up to a point in the past, emphasizing duration. Signal words: for, since, all day, when
Simple Future Tense
(+) S + will + V1 + O  |  (-) S + will not (won't) + V1  |  (?) Will + S + V1?
+ Positive
  • She will write the exam tomorrow.
  • They will visit us next week.
  • I will help you with this.
- Negative
  • She will not write the exam.
  • He won't attend the meeting.
? Interrogative
  • Will she write the exam?
  • Will you help me?
Uses: Predictions, spontaneous decisions, promises, offers, future facts. Signal words: tomorrow, next week/month/year, soon, in future, one day
Future Continuous Tense
(+) S + will + be + V-ing + O  |  (-) S + will not + be + V-ing  |  (?) Will + S + be + V-ing?
+ Positive
  • She will be working at 9 p.m.
  • I will be sleeping by midnight.
  • They will be travelling next week.
- Negative
  • She will not be working tonight.
? Interrogative
  • Will she be working at 9?
Uses: Ongoing action at a specific future time, planned events. Signal words: at this time tomorrow, at 8 p.m. tonight, this time next week
Future Perfect Tense
(+) S + will + have + V3 + O  |  (-) S + will not + have + V3  |  (?) Will + S + have + V3?
+ Positive
  • She will have finished the project by Friday.
  • They will have left before you arrive.
- Negative
  • He won't have reached by noon.
? Interrogative
  • Will she have finished by Friday?
Uses: Action completed before a specific future time. Signal words: by, by the time, before (+ future point)
Future Perfect Continuous Tense
(+) S + will + have + been + V-ing + O  |  (-) S + will + not + have + been + V-ing  |  (?) Will + S + have + been + V-ing?
+ Positive
  • By 2025, she will have been teaching for 10 years.
  • I will have been waiting for 2 hours by 5 p.m.
  • By March, he will have been running for 3 months.
- Negative
  • By then, she will not have been working long enough.
  • He won't have been studying for even a week.
  • They will not have been living here for long.
? Interrogative
  • Will she have been teaching for 10 years?
  • How long will he have been waiting?
  • Will they have been living here long?
Uses: Continuous action ongoing up to a specific future time, emphasizing duration. Signal words: for + time, by, by the time

Voice

Active & Passive Voice

Transform sentences from active to passive voice across all tenses.

Active Voice

The subject performs the action.

Structure: Subject + Verb + Object
  • The cat caught the mouse.
  • She is writing a letter.
  • They have built the bridge.

Passive Voice

The object of active becomes subject; subject goes to end with "by".

Structure: Object + be (correct form) + V3 + by + Subject
  • The mouse was caught by the cat.
  • A letter is being written by her.
  • The bridge has been built by them.

Passive Conversion Chart

TenseActivePassive
Simple PresentShe writes a letter.A letter is written by her.
Present ContinuousShe is writing a letter.A letter is being written by her.
Present PerfectShe has written a letter.A letter has been written by her.
Simple PastShe wrote a letter.A letter was written by her.
Past ContinuousShe was writing a letter.A letter was being written by her.
Past PerfectShe had written a letter.A letter had been written by her.
Simple FutureShe will write a letter.A letter will be written by her.
Modal (can/must)She can write a letter.A letter can be written by her.
Pronoun Changes: I→me | we→us | he→him | she→her | they→them
When to Use Passive: When the doer is unknown ("The wallet was stolen."), unimportant, or in formal/scientific writing.

He said that he was happy.

Articles

Articles: A, An, The & Zero Article

A

Before consonant sounds. Singular countable nouns.

  • a book, a car
  • a university (/j/ sound)
  • a one-way street (/w/ sound)

An

Before vowel sounds.

  • an apple, an egg, an idea
  • an hour (silent h)
  • an MBA, an X-ray

The

Definite article — specific/known noun.

  • the sun, the moon, the Earth
  • the Taj Mahal, the Ganges
  • the best, the first

Zero Article

No article used.

  • proper nouns: India, Paris
  • languages: English, Hindi
  • sports: play cricket
  • meals: have breakfast
A vs An — Sound Rule (NOT spelling):
• a university (/juː/ consonant sound)
• an hour (/aʊ/ — h is silent)
• an MLA (/em/ vowel sound)
Common Mistakes:
✗ I am a honest person → ✓ an honest person
✗ I play the cricket → ✓ I play cricket (sports take no article)
✗ The gold is expensive → ✓ Gold is expensive (general)
Grammar Rule

Subject-Verb Agreement

The verb must agree with the subject in number and person.

A singular subject takes a singular verb.
The dog barks. | She plays tennis.
The dogs bark. | Birds fly high.
Ram and Shyam are friends. | Bread and butter are my breakfast.
Exception: "The poet and singer is arriving." (same person)
Either you or he is wrong. | Neither he nor they are responsible.
As a unit → singular: "The team is ready." | Individually → plural: "The team are arguing."
Everyone is invited. | Each of the students has a book.
The news is shocking. | Mathematics is my favourite subject.
There is a book on the table. | There are many books on the shelf.
Half of the students are present. | Half of the bread is eaten.
Ten kilometres is a long distance. | Five hundred rupees is enough.
Modals

Modal Auxiliary Verbs

Modals express ability, possibility, permission, obligation, and more. Always followed by V1 (base verb).

Can / Could

  • She can speak five languages. (ability)
  • Can I use your phone? (permission)
  • He could swim as a child. (past ability)
  • Could you help me? (polite request)
  • It could rain. (possibility)

May / Might

  • May I come in? (formal permission)
  • It may rain tonight. (~50% possibility)
  • She might come to the party. (~30%)
  • You might try a different approach. (suggestion)

Shall / Should

  • Shall we start? (offer/suggestion)
  • You should exercise daily. (advice)
  • You should apologise. (moral obligation)
  • She should be here by now. (expectation)

Will / Would

  • I will call you tomorrow. (future)
  • Would you like some tea? (polite)
  • He would walk to school as a child. (past habit)
  • I would help if I could. (conditional)

Must / Ought To

  • You must wear a seatbelt. (strong obligation)
  • She must be very tired. (deduction)
  • You must not smoke here. (prohibition)
  • You ought to respect your elders. (moral duty)

Need / Used To / Dare

  • You needn't shout; I can hear. (not necessary)
  • He used to smoke, but he quit. (past habit)
  • Dare you challenge me? (challenge)
  • She needs to see a doctor. (necessity)
Sentences

Types of Sentences

Sentences are classified based on their function and structure.

Assertive (Declarative)

States a fact or opinion. Ends with full stop.

  • The Earth is round.
  • She went to school yesterday.
  • I love reading books.

Interrogative

Asks a question. Ends with question mark.

  • Where do you live?
  • Have you eaten?
  • Is she coming tomorrow?

Imperative

Command, request, advice. Subject "you" implied.

  • Close the door.
  • Please sit down.
  • Do not touch the wire.

Exclamatory

Expresses strong emotion. Ends with exclamation mark.

  • What a beautiful sunset!
  • How clever she is!
  • What nonsense!

Optative

Expresses a wish, prayer, or desire.

  • May you live long!
  • May God bless you!
  • Long live the king!
Clauses & Phrases

Clauses & Phrases

Clauses

Noun Clause

Acts as a noun. Begins with: that, what, who, whether, how, why.

  • What she said surprised everyone. (Subject)
  • I know that she is honest. (Object)
  • The fact is that he lied. (Complement)

Adjective Clause

Modifies a noun. Begins with: who, whom, whose, which, that.

  • The man who called you is my uncle.
  • The book that I read was interesting.
  • The girl whose bag was stolen is crying.

Adverb Clause

Modifies a verb. Tells when, where, why, how, condition.

  • She left before he arrived. (time)
  • He failed because he was lazy. (reason)
  • I'll help you if I am free. (condition)

Phrases

Noun Phrase

  • The tall man in blue is my father.
  • I love reading mystery novels.

Verb Phrase

  • She has been sleeping for hours.
  • He will have finished by noon.

Adjective Phrase

  • The girl with golden hair is beautiful.
  • A man of great courage stood up.

Adverb Phrase

  • She speaks in a soft voice.
  • He arrived at the crack of dawn.
Conditionals

Conditional Sentences

Conditional sentences express "if" situations and their results.

Zero Conditional — General Truths
If + Simple Present, Simple Present
  • If you heat water to 100°C, it boils.
  • If you don't eat, you get hungry.
  • Plants die if they don't get water.
First Conditional — Real / Likely Future
If + Simple Present, will + V1
  • If it rains, I will stay home.
  • If you study hard, you will pass.
  • She will be happy if you call her.
Second Conditional — Unreal / Hypothetical Present
If + Simple Past, would + V1
  • If I were rich, I would travel the world.
  • If she knew, she would tell you.
Use "were" for all persons: If I were..., If he were...
Third Conditional — Unreal / Hypothetical Past
If + Past Perfect, would have + V3
  • If I had studied, I would have passed.

The Importance of Trees

Trees are the lungs of the Earth. They absorb carbon dioxide from the atmosphere and release oxygen, making life possible. A single tree can provide enough oxygen for two human beings to breathe for a whole year. Trees also regulate the climate by absorbing heat and releasing water vapour through a process called transpiration. Forests cover about 31% of the Earth's surface and are home to more than 80% of the world's terrestrial biodiversity. However, deforestation — the large-scale cutting of trees — poses a serious threat. Every year, an area of forest equivalent to the size of Portugal is destroyed. This not only reduces oxygen levels but also leads to soil erosion, flooding, loss of wildlife habitats, and accelerated climate change. Planting trees, therefore, is not just an environmental act but a survival strategy for humanity.

The Power of Habit

Habits are the invisible architecture of daily life. Researchers estimate that 40 to 45 percent of what we do every day feels like a decision, but it is actually a habit. The brain loves habits because they save energy — once a behaviour becomes automatic, the brain can focus on other tasks. Habits form through a three-step loop: a cue (a trigger that tells the brain to go into automatic mode), a routine (the behaviour itself), and a reward (which tells the brain whether this loop is worth remembering for the future). Understanding this loop is the key to changing habits. To break a bad habit, you must keep the old cue and reward but insert a new routine. For example, if stress (cue) leads to eating junk food (routine) for the pleasure it brings (reward), you can replace junk food with a walk or meditation while keeping the same cue and reward. Building good habits is not about willpower — it is about designing your environment.

Questions & Answers

Answer: The three steps are: (1) Cue — a trigger that starts automatic behaviour; (2) Routine — the behaviour itself; (3) Reward — what reinforces the loop in the brain.
Answer: To change a bad habit, keep the old cue and reward but replace the routine with a new, healthier behaviour.
Answer: "Invisible architecture" is a metaphor meaning that habits invisibly structure and shape our daily lives, just as architecture gives shape to a building.
Mahatma Gandhi — Father of the Nation

Mohandas Karamchand Gandhi, popularly known as Mahatma Gandhi, was born on 2 October 1869 in Porbandar, Gujarat. He was a lawyer, political leader, and social activist who became the foremost leader of India's independence movement against British rule. Gandhi's philosophy of non-violent resistance, which he called Satyagraha (truth-force), profoundly influenced movements for civil rights and freedom across the world. He led numerous campaigns including the Non-Cooperation Movement (1920), the Civil Disobedience Movement (1930), and the Quit India Movement (1942). His famous Dandi March of 1930, a 240-mile walk to the sea to protest against the British salt tax, became a symbol of peaceful resistance worldwide. Gandhi believed in the equality of all religions and worked tirelessly for the eradication of untouchability. On 30 January 1948, he was assassinated by Nathuram Godse. His birthday, 2 October, is celebrated as the International Day of Non-Violence by the United Nations.

Narrations

Narrations

Learn how to narrate stories, events, and experiences effectively using proper structure and connectors.

What is Narration?

Narration is the act of telling a story or describing a series of events. A narrator communicates events to the reader or listener in a structured way with a clear beginning, middle, and end.

Types of Narration

  • First Person: Narrator is part of the story. Uses "I, me, we". e.g., "I walked into the room..."
  • Second Person: Addresses reader directly. Uses "you". e.g., "You open the door..."
  • Third Person: Narrator is outside the story. Uses "he, she, they". e.g., "She walked into the room..."

Narrative Point of View

  • Omniscient: Narrator knows all thoughts, feelings, and events.
  • Limited: Narrator knows only one character's thoughts.
  • Objective: Narrator reports only what is seen/heard — no inner thoughts.
  • Unreliable: Narrator's account may be biased or incorrect.

Elements of a Good Narration

  • Setting: Time and place of the story.
  • Characters: People involved in the events.
  • Plot: Sequence of events.
  • Conflict: Problem or challenge faced.
  • Resolution: How the conflict is solved.
  • Theme: Central message or lesson.
Reported Speech & Narration: In narration of speech (indirect speech), we change pronouns, tense, and time expressions. E.g., "I will go tomorrow" → He said that he would go the next day.

1. Introduction (Setting)

Introduce the characters, setting (time & place), and the initial situation. Hook the reader's attention.

Example: "It was a stormy night in December when Ravi found an old, dusty letter under his grandfather's bed."
2. Rising Action

Events build up tension. The main character faces challenges. Use sequence connectors to link events.

Example: "As he read the letter, his hands trembled. The letter spoke of a hidden treasure — and a dark secret his family had kept for decades."
3. Climax

The highest point of tension. The turning point of the story where the main conflict reaches its peak.

Example: "Suddenly, the lights went off. Ravi heard footsteps. Someone else had found out about the letter!"
4. Resolution

The conflict is resolved. Loose ends are tied up. The story comes to a satisfying conclusion with a lesson/moral.

Example: "In the end, Ravi realised that the treasure was not gold — it was a collection of letters that taught him the value of honesty and family bonds."
Narration Tenses: Past simple is the most common tense in narration (he walked, she said). Past continuous is used for background actions (the rain was falling). Past perfect shows earlier events (he had already left).

Tips for Effective Narration

Show, don't tell

Instead of "He was scared", write "His heart pounded and his palms went cold."

Use dialogue

Bring characters to life with direct speech. "Don't go there!" she warned.

Vivid descriptions

Use sensory details — what was seen, heard, smelt, felt, and tasted.

Logical flow

Use connectors to link events. Avoid jumping between time frames without signals.

Consistent tense

Stick to past or present tense throughout. Switching randomly confuses the reader.

Strong ending

End with a moral, reflection, or twist that leaves a lasting impression.

Sample 1 — First Person Narration (Personal Experience)

It was the morning of my final examination, and I had barely slept the night before. Setting

First of all, I woke up early and revised my notes. Then, I had a quick breakfast and set off to school. Rising Action

As I entered the hall, I realised I had left my admit card at home. My heart sank. All of a sudden, I saw my friend waving at me — she had an extra copy. Climax → Relief

Eventually, I sat down, took a deep breath, and wrote my best exam ever. In the end, all my hard work paid off when I scored 98 out of 100. Resolution

Moral: Panic never helps. Stay calm and trust your preparation.

Sample 2 — Third Person Narration (Short Story)

Once upon a time, in a small village near the hills, there lived a young girl named Priya who loved to paint. Introduction

One day, as she was walking through the forest, she found an old injured sparrow. She gently picked it up and took it home. Day after day, she fed it and cared for it. Rising Action

After a few weeks, the sparrow recovered. But when Priya opened the cage, the sparrow did not fly away. It chirped sweetly and perched on her shoulder. Climax

From that day on, the sparrow became Priya's closest companion, inspiring her paintings and reminding her that kindness always returns. Resolution

Moral: Kindness towards living beings always comes back to us in beautiful ways.

Sample 3 — Narration of an Event (Formal/Descriptive)

On the 15th of August, our school organised a grand Independence Day celebration. Introduction

Initially, the programme began with the hoisting of the national flag by the principal, accompanied by the national anthem. Subsequently, students from various classes performed patriotic songs, dances, and skits depicting India's struggle for freedom. Sequence of Events

The highlight of the event was an inspiring speech by our school's oldest alumna, who spoke about Mahatma Gandhi's vision for India. The audience was visibly moved. Climax / Highlight

Finally, sweets were distributed among all students and staff. The celebration concluded with a unanimous pledge to serve our nation with honesty and dedication. Conclusion

Intonation Tip: In English, rising intonation (↑) is used for yes/no questions. Falling intonation (↓) is used for statements and wh-questions. E.g., "Are you coming? ↑" vs "I am coming ↓."

Saying No Politely

  • I'm afraid I'm not available at that time.
  • I'd love to, but I have a prior commitment.
  • Unfortunately, that won't be possible.
  • I'm sorry, but I have to decline.
  • Thank you for the offer, but I'll have to pass.
  • I appreciate it, but I can't commit right now.

Agreeing & Disagreeing

  • Agree: Absolutely! · Certainly! · That's a good point.
  • Partly agree: I see what you mean, but... · Up to a point, yes...
  • Disagree politely: I'm not entirely sure I agree. · With all due respect... · I see it differently.
  • Neutral: That's an interesting perspective. · I can see why you'd think that.

Making a Business Call

Caller (Amit)
Good morning. Could I speak to Ms. Priya Singh, please?
Receptionist
Good morning! May I ask who's calling?
Amit
Yes, this is Amit Sharma from ABC Technologies.
Receptionist
Please hold for a moment, Mr. Sharma. I'll transfer your call.
Amit
Thank you.
Priya
Hello, this is Priya Singh speaking. How can I help you?
Amit
Hello Ms. Singh, I'm calling regarding the proposal we sent last week. I was wondering if you had a chance to review it.
Priya
Yes, I've gone through it. Could we schedule a meeting to discuss the details?
Amit
Absolutely. Would Thursday afternoon work for you?
Priya
Thursday at 3 PM suits me perfectly. I'll send a calendar invite.
Amit
Wonderful. Thank you, Ms. Singh. Looking forward to it. Have a good day.

Key Telephone Phrases

Answering a Call
  • "Hello, [Company Name], [Your Name] speaking."
  • "Good morning, how may I direct your call?"
  • "Thank you for calling. How can I assist you?"
When You Can't Hear
  • "I'm sorry, could you speak up a little?"
  • "I'm afraid the line is not very clear."
  • "Could you repeat that, please?"
  • "I didn't quite catch that."
Taking a Message
  • "I'm afraid she's not available at the moment."
  • "Can I take a message?"
  • "I'll make sure she gets your message."
  • "Could I have your contact number?"
Ending a Call
  • "Thank you for calling. Have a great day."
  • "I'll get back to you shortly."
  • "It was lovely speaking with you."
  • "Goodbye and take care."
Use formal, confident, and clear language in interviews. Avoid slang. Structure your answers using the STAR method: Situation, Task, Action, Result.

Mock Interview Conversation

Interviewer
Good morning. Please have a seat. Tell me about yourself.
Candidate
Good morning, thank you for having me. I'm Rahul Sharma, a Computer Science graduate from Delhi University. I have two years of experience in software development and I'm passionate about building efficient and user-friendly applications.
Interviewer
Why do you want to work with our company?
Candidate
I've always admired your company's commitment to innovation and your work culture. I believe this role aligns perfectly with my skills in Java and cloud computing, and I am eager to contribute to your team's goals.
Interviewer
What is your greatest weakness?
Candidate
I tend to be overly detail-oriented, which sometimes slows me down. However, I've been actively working on prioritising tasks and managing time more effectively, which has significantly improved my productivity.
Interviewer
Where do you see yourself in five years?
Candidate
I envision myself in a senior technical role, leading projects and mentoring junior developers. I'd love to grow with this organisation and contribute to its long-term vision.
