🔬 Introduction to Science

What is Science?

Science is a systematic and logical approach to discovering how things in the universe work. It is a process of acquiring knowledge through observation, experimentation, and evidence-based reasoning.

Key Features of Science

  • Empirical: Based on observations and experiments
  • Systematic: Follows a structured method
  • Reproducible: Experiments can be repeated
  • Falsifiable: Claims can be tested and disproved
  • Self-correcting: Updates based on new evidence

Types of Science

TypeDescriptionExamples
Natural ScienceStudy of natural phenomenaPhysics, Chemistry, Biology
Social ScienceStudy of human societyPsychology, Sociology
Formal ScienceLogical/mathematical systemsMathematics, Logic
Applied SciencePractical applicationsEngineering, Medicine
Q1. Which of the following is NOT a characteristic of science?
✅ Correct! Science does not rely on authority — it relies on evidence and experimentation. "Based on authority alone" is not a feature of science.

The Scientific Method

The scientific method is a step-by-step process used by scientists to investigate questions and find answers.

1
Observation
Notice and describe a phenomenon or problem in the natural world
2
Question
Ask a specific, testable question about the observation
3
Hypothesis
Form a testable prediction or explanation for the phenomenon
4
Experiment
Design and conduct a controlled experiment to test the hypothesis
5
Data Analysis
Collect, organize, and analyze results from the experiment
6
Conclusion
Draw conclusions — does the data support or refute the hypothesis?
7
Communication
Share results with the scientific community for peer review
💡 Key Terms: Independent variable (what you change), Dependent variable (what you measure), Control variable (what you keep the same).

Mechanics — Motion & Forces

Distance vs. Displacement

DistanceDisplacement
Total path length travelledShortest path from start to end
Scalar quantityVector quantity
Always positiveCan be +, −, or zero

Speed, Velocity & Acceleration

Speed
Speed = Distance / Time  |  v = d/t
Unit: m/s (SI) — Scalar quantity
Velocity
Velocity = Displacement / Time  |  v = s/t
Unit: m/s — Vector quantity
Acceleration
a = (v - u) / t
u = initial velocity, v = final velocity, t = time. Unit: m/s²

Equations of Motion (Uniformly Accelerated)

v = u + at
1st equation — relates velocity and time
s = ut + ½at²
2nd equation — relates distance and time
v² = u² + 2as
3rd equation — velocity without time

Newton's Laws of Motion

1st Law (Law of Inertia): An object at rest stays at rest and an object in motion stays in motion unless acted upon by an external force.
2nd Law: Force = Mass × Acceleration  |  F = ma
The acceleration of an object is directly proportional to the net force and inversely proportional to its mass.
3rd Law: For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction.
Example: Rocket propulsion, swimming, gun recoil.

Gravitation

Universal Law of Gravitation (Newton)
F = G × m₁ × m₂ / r²
G = 6.674 × 10⁻¹¹ N·m²/kg² — Gravitational constant
Acceleration due to gravity
g = 9.8 m/s² (on Earth's surface)
g on Moon ≈ 1.6 m/s² (1/6 of Earth)

Friction

f = μN
μ = coefficient of friction, N = Normal force
TypeDescription
Static FrictionPrevents motion when object is at rest
Kinetic FrictionOpposes motion when object is sliding
Rolling FrictionOpposes rolling (least among the three)

Circular Motion

Centripetal Force
F = mv²/r
m = mass, v = speed, r = radius of circular path

Work, Power & Energy

Work Done
W = F × d × cosθ
F = force, d = displacement, θ = angle between force and displacement. Unit: Joule (J)
Kinetic Energy
KE = ½mv²
m = mass (kg), v = velocity (m/s). Unit: Joule (J)
Potential Energy (Gravitational)
PE = mgh
m = mass, g = 9.8 m/s², h = height. Unit: Joule (J)
Power
P = W/t = F×v
Unit: Watt (W) = J/s. 1 HP = 746 W

Law of Conservation of Energy

Energy cannot be created or destroyed; it can only be converted from one form to another.

Total Mechanical Energy = KE + PE = constant (in absence of friction)

Types of Energy

TypeExample
Kinetic EnergyMoving car, flowing water
Potential EnergyStretched spring, raised object
Thermal EnergyHot water, fire
Chemical EnergyFood, fuel, batteries
Nuclear EnergyNuclear reactor, Sun
Electrical EnergyElectricity in wires
Light EnergySunlight, laser
Sound EnergyMusic, thunder

Heat & Thermodynamics

Temperature Scales

°C = (°F − 32) × 5/9   |   K = °C + 273.15

Heat Transfer

ModeMedium RequiredExample
ConductionSolid (contact)Metal rod heating up
ConvectionLiquid/GasBoiling water, sea breeze
RadiationNot requiredSunlight, microwave oven

Laws of Thermodynamics

0th Law: If A is in thermal equilibrium with B, and B with C, then A is in equilibrium with C.
1st Law: Energy is conserved. ΔU = Q − W (change in internal energy = heat added − work done)
2nd Law: Heat flows from hot to cold spontaneously. Entropy of an isolated system always increases.
3rd Law: As temperature approaches absolute zero (0 K), entropy approaches a minimum value.

Specific Heat Capacity

Q = mcΔT
Q = heat, m = mass, c = specific heat capacity, ΔT = temperature change
c of water = 4200 J/kg°C

Sound

Sound is a mechanical longitudinal wave that requires a medium for propagation. It cannot travel through vacuum.

Wave Speed
v = fλ
v = wave speed (m/s), f = frequency (Hz), λ = wavelength (m)
Speed of Sound in Air
v ≈ 343 m/s at 20°C
Increases with temperature. Sound travels fastest in solids.

Properties of Sound

PropertyRelated toUnit
FrequencyPitch (high/low)Hertz (Hz)
AmplitudeLoudness (decibels)dB
WavelengthDistance per cyclem
Time PeriodT = 1/fseconds (s)

Echo & SONAR

Echo: Reflection of sound. Heard when surface is ≥17m away (minimum distance for echo in air).
SONAR: Sound Navigation And Ranging. Uses ultrasonic waves to detect underwater objects.
Distance = (v × t) / 2

Frequency Ranges

Infrasonic< 20 HzEarthquakes, elephant communication

Ultrasonic> 20,000 HzBats, SONAR, medical imaging

Light & Optics

Light is an electromagnetic wave. Speed of light in vacuum: c = 3 × 10⁸ m/s

Laws of Reflection

  • Angle of incidence = Angle of reflection (∠i = ∠r)
  • Incident ray, reflected ray, and normal lie in the same plane

Laws of Refraction (Snell's Law)

n₁ sinθ₁ = n₂ sinθ₂
n = refractive index. Light bends toward normal when entering denser medium.
Refractive Index
n = c/v = sin i / sin r
n of glass ≈ 1.5, n of water ≈ 1.33, n of air ≈ 1

Mirror Formula

1/f = 1/v + 1/u
f = focal length, v = image distance, u = object distance. Magnification m = -v/u

Lens Formula

1/f = 1/v − 1/u
Power of lens P = 1/f (in metres). Unit: Dioptre (D)

Mirrors vs. Lenses

FeatureConcave MirrorConvex Mirror
Image typeReal/VirtualAlways virtual
UseShaving, headlights, solar furnaceRear-view mirrors
CentreReflects inwardReflects outward

Optical Phenomena

  • Total Internal Reflection: When light hits a boundary at > critical angle — used in fiber optics
  • Dispersion: White light splits into VIBGYOR spectrum through a prism
  • Scattering: Why the sky is blue (Rayleigh scattering)
  • Rainbow: Dispersion + internal reflection in water droplets

Electricity & Magnetism

Ohm's Law
V = IR
V = Voltage (Volts), I = Current (Amperes), R = Resistance (Ohms Ω)
Electric Power
P = VI = I²R = V²/R
Unit: Watt (W). 1 kWh = 3.6 × 10⁶ J
Resistance in Series
R_total = R₁ + R₂ + R₃ + ...
Resistance in Parallel
1/R_total = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂ + 1/R₃ + ...

Key Terms

TermDefinitionUnit
Current (I)Flow of electric charge per secondAmpere (A)
Voltage (V)Work done per unit chargeVolt (V)
Resistance (R)Opposition to current flowOhm (Ω)
Capacitance (C)Ability to store chargeFarad (F)
Inductance (L)Opposition to change in currentHenry (H)

Magnetism

  • Magnetic Field (B): Region around a magnet where magnetic force acts. Unit: Tesla (T)
  • Electromagnets: Temporary magnets created by electric current through a coil
  • Fleming's Left-Hand Rule: Thumb = Force, Index = Field, Middle = Current (for motors)
  • Fleming's Right-Hand Rule: For generators (electromagnetic induction)
  • Faraday's Law: EMF = −dΦ/dt (rate of change of magnetic flux)

Einstein's Theory of Relativity

Mass-Energy Equivalence (Einstein)
E = mc²
E = energy (J), m = mass (kg), c = speed of light = 3×10⁸ m/s
Special Relativity (1905): Laws of physics are the same in all inertial frames. The speed of light is constant for all observers.

General Relativity (1915): Gravity is the curvature of spacetime caused by mass and energy.

Nuclear Physics

Radioactive Decay
N = N₀ × e^(−λt)
N₀ = initial nuclei, λ = decay constant, t = time. Half-life: t½ = 0.693/λ

Quantum Mechanics (Basics)

Planck's Equation
E = hf
h = Planck's constant = 6.626 × 10⁻³⁴ J·s, f = frequency
de Broglie Wavelength
λ = h / mv
Matter also behaves like a wave at quantum scale
  • Heisenberg's Uncertainty Principle: Δx · Δp ≥ ℏ/2 — Cannot simultaneously know exact position and momentum
  • Wave-Particle Duality: Light and matter exhibit both wave and particle properties
  • Photoelectric Effect: Light ejects electrons from metal surface — proved by Einstein (1905)

Matter & States

Matter is anything that has mass and occupies space. It exists in different states based on arrangement and movement of particles.

PropertySolidLiquidGasPlasma
ShapeFixedIndefiniteIndefiniteIndefinite
VolumeFixedFixedIndefiniteIndefinite
Particle arrangementTightly packedLoosely packedFar apartIonized
CompressibilityVery lowLowHigh—
ExampleIce, ironWater, mercuryOxygen, airStars, lightning

Changes of State

Solid→Liquid: Melting | Liquid→Solid: Freezing
Liquid→Gas: Vaporization | Gas→Liquid: Condensation
Solid→Gas: Sublimation (e.g. dry ice, iodine) | Gas→Solid: Deposition

Classification of Matter

  • Pure Substance: Fixed composition — Elements (H₂, O₂) and Compounds (H₂O, NaCl)
  • Mixture: Variable composition — Homogeneous (solution) and Heterogeneous (suspension, colloid)

Atomic Structure

Models of Atom

1803
Dalton's Model
Atom is a solid indivisible sphere. Different atoms for different elements.
1897
Thomson's Model (Plum Pudding)
Electrons embedded in a positive sphere like plums in pudding.
1911
Rutherford's Model (Nuclear)
Dense positive nucleus at center; electrons revolve around it.
1913
Bohr's Model
Electrons occupy fixed energy orbits (shells). Energy is quantized.
1926
Quantum Model
Electron cloud / probability density model based on wave mechanics.
