📊 GDP & Economic Basics

Understanding GDP

ConceptDefinition
GDP (Gross Domestic Product)Total monetary value of all goods & services produced within a country in a year. Includes output by all residents (foreign + domestic) within boundaries.
GNP (Gross National Product)GDP + income earned by residents abroad – income earned by foreigners in India. Focuses on nationality, not location.
NNP at Market PriceGNP – Depreciation
NNP at Factor Cost (National Income)NNP at Market Price – Net Indirect Taxes
GDP at Constant Prices (Real GDP)GDP adjusted for inflation; measures actual growth; used to compare across years
GDP at Current Prices (Nominal GDP)GDP measured at current year prices; affected by both growth AND inflation
GDP DeflatorRatio of Nominal GDP to Real GDP × 100; measures price level changes
Base Year (India)2011-12 is the current base year for GDP calculation (changed from 2004-05)

Measuring GDP — Three Approaches

  • Output/Production Method: Sum of value added by all sectors (Agriculture + Industry + Services)
  • Income Method: Sum of all incomes earned (wages + rent + interest + profit)
  • Expenditure Method: C + I + G + (X–M) — Consumption + Investment + Government Spending + Net Exports

National Income Accounting — Formula Chain

ConceptFormulaKey Point
GDP (at Market Price)C + I + G + (X − M)C=Private Consumption, I=Investment, G=Govt Spending, X=Exports, M=Imports
GNP (at Market Price)GDP + Net Factor Income from Abroad (NFIA)NFIA = Income earned by Indians abroad − Income earned by foreigners in India
NNP (at Market Price)GNP − Depreciation (Capital Consumption)Depreciation = wear & tear on capital goods
NNP (at Factor Cost) = National IncomeNNP at MP − Net Indirect TaxesNet Indirect Taxes = Indirect Taxes − Subsidies
Per Capita IncomeNational Income ÷ Total PopulationBest measure of average living standard
GDP Deflator(Nominal GDP ÷ Real GDP) × 100Broadest inflation measure; covers all goods & services in economy

Expenditure Method — GDP Components (C + I + G + X − M)

C — Private Final Consumption Expenditure: Household spending on goods & services; largest component (~55–60% of India's GDP)

I — Gross Fixed Capital Formation (GFCF): Investment in machinery, buildings, infrastructure (~30% of GDP); key driver of long-term growth

G — Government Final Consumption Expenditure: Govt spending on salaries, goods, services (excludes transfer payments like subsidies)

X − M — Net Exports: Exports minus Imports; India typically has a negative net export (trade deficit), dragging GDP slightly

Key Monetary Policy Rates

RateDefinitionCurrent (approx)
Repo RateRate at which RBI lends to commercial banks (short-term, against securities)6.25% (cut Feb 2025 from 6.5%)
Reverse Repo RateRate at which RBI borrows from commercial banks (against securities)3.35% (SDF now used as de-facto floor)
SDF (Standing Deposit Facility)Floor of interest rate corridor; banks park excess funds with RBI; no collateral needed from banks6.00% (25 bps below repo)
MSF (Marginal Standing Facility)Emergency overnight borrowing facility for banks; 25 bps above repo rate; ceiling of corridor6.50%
Bank RateRate at which RBI lends without collateral (long-term); aligned with MSF rate6.50%
CRR (Cash Reserve Ratio)% of deposits banks must keep as cash with RBI; no interest earned; RBI's quantitative tool4%
SLR (Statutory Liquidity Ratio)% of deposits banks must maintain in gold/govt-approved securities; earning asset18%
📌 Monetary Policy Committee (MPC): 6-member committee (3 RBI + 3 Govt nominees); meets 6 times/year; decides Repo Rate by majority vote; Governor has casting vote. Target: CPI inflation 4% (±2%). Constituted 2016 under RBI Amendment Act.

Credit Control Tools of RBI

TypeToolHow it Works
Quantitative Tools

CRRHigher CRR → banks park more cash with RBI → less money to lend
SLRHigher SLR → banks hold more in govt securities → reduced lending capacity

RBI's Key Functions in Detail

FunctionDetail
Currency IssuanceSole authority to issue notes (except ₹1 coin/note — issued by Govt); Minimum Reserve System since 1956: must hold ₹200 cr (₹115 cr in gold)
Banker to the GovernmentManages Govt of India accounts; conducts Govt borrowing programme; transfers surplus profit to Govt (Bimal Jalan Committee 2019 set transfer framework)
Banker's Bank / Lender of Last ResortHolds CRR/SLR of all banks; provides emergency funds (MSF); clears inter-bank transactions via RTGS
Credit RegulationFormulates monetary policy; controls money supply through repo, CRR, SLR, OMOs
Foreign Exchange ManagementManages forex reserves; administers FEMA 1999; intervenes in forex market to prevent excess volatility
Regulation & SupervisionLicenses banks; sets prudential norms (Capital Adequacy Ratio); Prompt Corrective Action (PCA) for weak banks
Developmental FunctionsFinancial inclusion norms; payment systems oversight (RTGS, NEFT, UPI via NPCI subsidiary); priority sector guidelines
📌 Interest Rate Corridor: RBI operates a corridor system — SDF Rate (floor, 6.00%) ↔ Repo Rate (policy rate, 6.25%) ↔ MSF Rate (ceiling, 6.50%). Banks borrow at repo, park excess at SDF. The corridor width is ±25 bps around the repo rate. Scheduled Banks are those listed in the 2nd Schedule of the RBI Act — eligible for RBI loans and have high public confidence.

Priority Sector Lending (PSL)

Banks must lend 40% of Adjusted Net Bank Credit (ANBC) to priority sectors. Includes: Agriculture (18%), Micro & Small Enterprises, Education, Housing, Social Infrastructure, Renewable Energy, Weaker Sections (10%), Export Credit.

📌 PSB Consolidation 2019-20: 10 public sector banks merged into 4 — Vijaya Bank + Dena Bank → Bank of Baroda; OBC + UBI → PNB; Andhra Bank + Corporation Bank → Union Bank; Syndicate Bank → Canara Bank. Total PSBs reduced from 27 to 12.

Basel Norms (International Banking Standards)

Basel FrameworkYearKey FocusIndia Status
Basel I1988 (BCBS)Minimum Capital Adequacy Ratio (CAR) of 8%; credit risk onlyImplemented 1992; India set 9% CAR
Basel II2004Three Pillars — Minimum Capital (Credit+Market+Op risk), Supervisory Review, Market DisciplineImplemented by RBI from 2009

📌 Capital Adequacy Ratio (CAR/CRAR): = (Tier 1 Capital + Tier 2 Capital) ÷ Risk Weighted Assets × 100. RBI mandates minimum 9% (higher than Basel III's 8%). Tier 1 = Core capital (equity, retained earnings). Tier 2 = Supplementary capital (subordinated debt, provisions).

📑 Union Budget

Understanding the Union Budget

TermDefinition
Annual Financial Statement (Art. 112)Constitutional name for the Union Budget; statement of receipts and expenditures for a financial year
PresentationFinance Minister presents on February 1 (changed from last working day of Feb since 2017)
Railway BudgetMerged with Union Budget from 2017

📌 FRBM Act (2003) — Fiscal Responsibility & Budget Management Act; targets fiscal consolidation. Original target: Fiscal Deficit ≤3% of GDP. Currently 5.1% target for FY2024-25; roadmap to 4.5% by FY2025-26.
