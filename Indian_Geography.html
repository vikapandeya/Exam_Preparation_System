Northern Plains (Indo-Gangetic-Brahmaputra Plains)

  • Stretches 2,400 km from Punjab in the west to Assam in the east; 240–320 km wide
  • Formed by alluvial deposits of three great river systems: Indus, Ganga, and Brahmaputra
  • Most fertile agricultural region — called the Granary of India
  • Khadar: new alluvium near river channels — very fertile, flood-prone, lighter in colour
  • Bhangar: old alluvium on higher ground — well-drained, contains kankar (limestone nodules)
  • Terai: marshy, forested strip along Himalayan foothills; drained for cultivation
  • Bhur: coarse alluvium sandy ridges in Ganga-Yamuna plains (UP)

Deccan Plateau

  • Triangular plateau bounded by Satpura range (north), Western Ghats (west), Eastern Ghats (east)
  • Average elevation: 600 m; tilts east → most rivers flow east into Bay of Bengal
  • Central Highlands: Malwa Plateau, Bundelkhand, Baghelkhand — north of Narmada
  • Southern Deccan Plateau includes Telangana, Karnataka, and Maharashtra interior plateaus
  • Deccan Trap: NW Deccan (Maharashtra, Gujarat, MP) covered by volcanic basalt (lava flows ~65 million years ago); source of black cotton (Regur) soil

📌 Deccan Trap: The northwestern Deccan plateau (Maharashtra, Gujarat, western MP) is covered by volcanic basalt rock formed ~65 million years ago by massive lava eruptions, possibly linked to the dinosaur extinction event. This basalt weathers into black cotton soil (Regur) — the most fertile soil for cotton cultivation. The Deccan Traps cover ~500,000 km², one of Earth's largest volcanic features. Lonar Lake (Maharashtra) is a crater lake formed by a meteorite impact in this basalt region.

📌 Indus Waters Treaty (1960): Signed between India and Pakistan with World Bank mediation by PM Nehru and Ayub Khan. India gets exclusive rights to three eastern rivers — Ravi, Beas, Sutlej. 📌 East-flowing vs West-flowing Peninsular Rivers: Most peninsular rivers flow east into Bay of Bengal and form large deltas (Mahanadi, Godavari, Krishna, Cauvery). Only Narmada and Tapi flow west through rift valleys into Arabian Sea — they form estuaries (not deltas) because they flow fast in hard rock. West-flowing rivers have shorter courses but steeper gradients — high hydroelectric potential. Godavari (1,465 km) is the longest peninsular river — called "Dakshin Ganga" (Ganga of the South) or "Vriddha Ganga" (Old Ganga).

Types of Rainfall in India

  • Convectional Rainfall: Hot regions — due to thermal heating; afternoon thundershowers (Assam, Bengal in summer)
  • Orographic (Relief) Rainfall: When moist winds rise over mountains — Western Ghats windward side, Himalayas foothills
  • Cyclonic Rainfall: Tropical cyclones in Bay of Bengal — affect Odisha, Andhra Pradesh, Tamil Nadu (Oct–Dec)
  • Western Disturbances: Extra-tropical cyclones originating in the Mediterranean Sea — bring winter rainfall and snowfall to NW India (Punjab, Haryana, UP, Uttarakhand, J&K, HP); crucial for Rabi crops (wheat, barley, mustard); also cause dense fog in Gangetic plains
📌 Monsoon Mechanism: SW Monsoon is caused by differential heating of land and sea. In summer, Indian landmass heats faster than Indian Ocean → low pressure over land → moist ocean winds rush in. The ITCZ (Inter-Tropical Convergence Zone) shifts north over India in June. Two branches: (1) Arabian Sea Branch — hits Western Ghats first, brings heavy rain to Kerala & Karnataka, crosses Deccan, reaches NW India. (2) Bay of Bengal Branch — enters NE India (Brahmaputra valley), moves west along Himalayas, merges with Arabian Sea branch over Gangetic Plains. Retreat: Monsoon withdraws from NW India by September 1, complete withdrawal from India by December.

📌 Soil Classification: ICAR (Indian Council of Agricultural Research) classifies Indian soils into 8 major orders. Alluvial soil is the most widespread (~43% of India's land) and most productive. Regur (Black) soil is unique for its self-ploughing nature — it swells when wet and cracks when dry, naturally mixing nutrients. India's soil survey is carried out by the National Bureau of Soil Survey & Land Use Planning (NBSS&LUP), headquartered at Nagpur. Soil erosion is the biggest threat — controlled by contour ploughing, terracing, windbreaks, and afforestation.

TopicDetail
Ozone LayerLocated in stratosphere (20–35 km altitude); absorbs harmful UV-B and UV-C radiation; measured in Dobson Units (DU); normal is 300 DU; critical for life on Earth

📌 ITCZ (Inter-Tropical Convergence Zone): A low-pressure belt near the equator where NE and SE trade winds converge; causes heavy convectional rainfall; migrates north in summer (forms the Indian Monsoon trough over Rajasthan) and south in winter. The movement of ITCZ northwards is a key trigger for the Indian SW Monsoon. Hadley Cell — equatorial air rises at ITCZ, moves poleward, descends at 30° (subtropical highs), returns as trade winds to equator. This circulation explains the desert belts at 20–30° latitude (Sahara, Thar, Arabian).
